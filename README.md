"What eco actions will you make today?"
---

Below are some actions for specific problems regarding climate change. In each chapter, there are solutions are then a "mindset part". You can use this list of solutions as a challenge - every day/month, choose one/a few and try to implement them into your life. 
The mindset part is about how I believe we should shift our focus and attention. How to think about this problem/ the actions you're doing. 



# 🌍 Global warming + Rising sea levels
- Search with Ecosia – a search engine which dedicates 100% of its profits for the planet – they plant trees, protect endangered species and generally spread knowledge about climate change. It’s free, fast and easy to switch. They also have an AI model, which uses renewable energy – ChatGPT and other fossil fuel powered AI models are quite harmful to the environment – data centers powered by fossil fuel energy make the Earth’s atmosphere warmer (as explained in previous chapters), also cooling down the technology takes huge amounts of water. [https://blog.ecosia.org/ecosia-financial-reports-tree-planting-receipts/] Using eco-friendly search engines (like Ecosia) plants trees with ad revenue. 1 search equals to approximately 0.5g of CO₂ absorbed – so 1,000 searches = 1 tree planted. ( ecosia.com )
- Use public transportation/ bike/ walk  (= less emissions) as much as you can. Taking a train instead of a car for medium-length distances would reduce emissions by around 80%. Using a train instead of a domestic flight would reduce your emissions by around 86%.[https://ourworldindata.org/travel-carbon-footprint] If you can, drive in Europe for your holidays by trains, buses or a car. A ferry is also a transportation with fewer emissions. Basically – try to reduce plane transportation. [https://archive.ourworldindata.org/20251125-173858/travel-carbon-footprint.html] 
- Change of energy supplier – (and again) fossil fuels are the leading contributors to greenhouse gas emissions which causes global warming and climate change. Making the switch to renewable energy supports the demand of it and lowers GHG emissions (and your carbon footprint). By switching to renewable energy, you can reduce your carbon footprint by up to 2.5 tons annually. [https://www.un.org/en/actnow/home-energy]
- Change of bank that’s not investing in fossil fuels – same as above. Switching to a green(er) bank ensures your money isn’t funding fossil fuels. The money we put into our bank accounts makes it possible for the bank to lend money to fossil fuel companies For example: the top 10 banks in North America each lend out between $20 billion and $40 billion to fossil fuel companies every year. [https://www.wired.com/story/your-money-is-funding-fossil-fuels-without-you-knowing-it/] Look for your bank on a website bank.green to see how much it invests in fossil fuels. [https://bank.green/]
- Unsubscribe email subscriptions you don’t want anymore – small step with small change, even though worldwide could be powerful. But at least you help lower the demand for energy (often from fossil fuels), which means fewer emissions and helps your digital mindfulness. It is estimated that one email ranges at between 0.03g and 26g of CO2e (CO2 equivalent). Globally, that is 0.3% of the world’s carbon footprint. [https://carbonliteracy.com/the-carbon-cost-of-an-email/]
- Switching to LED bulbs can reduce a household’s energy use by 75% compared to incandescent bulbs. [https://weismanelectric.com/the-benefits-of-switching-to-led-light-bulbs/]
- Unplugging "vampire devices" (=electronic devices plugged in that are not being used such as coffee machines, video game consoles and laptop chargers) can also lower your energy consumption. They use about 5 percent, maybe up to 10 percent of a total household's electricity. [https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/are-vampire-devices-draining-energy-in-your-home-heres-what-to-do]
- Vote and use your voice to spread knowledge.  “Vote with your wallet" by choosing products with eco-certifications (Fair Trade, EU Ecolabel,…). This sends a signal to markets and encourages sustainable practices. 

### MINDSET:
- This goes with every chapter. Don’t strive for perfection! Nobody is a perfect environmentalist! Our current systems aren’t made for sustainability so you sometimes realistically can’t be sustainable. 



# ♻️ Pollution 
- Start bins for plastic, paper, metal, glass (bonus points for biowaste bin!), recycle your electronics and batteries (you can also buy rechargeable batteries) properly. 
- Say no to plastic bags. If you do get them, reuse them somehow (you can wash them by hand with water and use again also). “Reduce, reuse, recycle” is a great mantra to remember and use. The EU generates 35.3 kg of plastic packaging waste per person annually  [https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/web/products-eurostat-news/w/ddn-20251022-1] If every person in the EU reduced their plastic waste by just 1 kg per year, it would prevent roughly 450,000 tons of avoided waste across the EU’s population of ~450 million people. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/] 
- If you have your own bottle, don’t be scared to buy drinks into it – there usually isn’t any problem about it.
- Avoid buying from the biggest plastic polluters – in 2024, it was: Cola-Cola, Nestlé, PepsiCo, Unilever, Procter & Gamble [https://www.shop-without-plastic.com/blogs/plastic-pollution-awareness/polluting-giants-the-5-most-polluting-consumer-goods-companies?srsltid=AfmBOopG40PtxIB_rOsf04awWVXl20y6tOr0YdfpZe5Gc9aOd0-PWu7_#ruffruff-table-of-contents-item-9].
- Bring your reusables (no matter how they look and where you go) – depending on the occasion you can bring a reusable bottle (or the plastic one you are reusing), your everyday tote/ shop bag, reusable produce bags (for fruits, veggies, pastry), cutlery (I would recommend some bamboo/ plastic, metal can be forbidden at some places), lunch box, straw (if you need one).
- Buy fruit and vegetables from stores into reusable bags. Choose groceries with less (plastic) packaging. 
- Other switches to avoid plastic/ microplastic – use wooden/ bamboo cutting board, try solid soap, shampoo or conditioner, drink tap water instead of bottled (tap water is cheaper, has less microplastics, usually the same quality as bottled [https://www.zerowaterfilter.com/blogs/news/is-bottled-water-really-better-than-tap-water?srsltid=AfmBOorkXzuWRBg2ezodqz1qiIwH5GE9nRD_pXObZTM981XatDq60ydL] ).In the kitchen, opt for reusable kitchen cloths and reusable silicon or coconut sponges. You can also invest in metal razor or reusable period products.
- Invest in non-toxic products for your face and body, learn which chemicals are harmful (they are apps like Safely, Alva or web pages for scanning toxic chemicals). I’m talking about body creams, makeup (I recommend brand ZAO – they’ve got clean, refillable makeup options), shower gels, shampoos,... Everything that goes on your skin.
- Visit farmers markets/ bulk stores (nearest from Benešov is probably Vlašim – [https://bezobaluvlasim.cz/sortiment/]). 
- (Don’t buy) what you can, make yourself – e.g. makeup remover cloth, wax napkins instead of plastic film/ aluminum foil. 
- Choose better materials in your clothing – e.g. organic cotton, hemp, bamboo, tencel. [19]
- Avoid chemicals in your clothes and look for known eco-labels – one of the strictest eco-label is GOTS, which certifies the entire supply chain including all aspects of production. It has two levels - more than 90 percent of organic cotton or more than 70 in the clothing. In other labels look for third-party testing (that’s way better than if a company tests/ regulates itself) or annual reports to find out more. [https://www.goingzerowaste.com/blog/sustainable-certifications/]
- Textiles are the largest source of primary microplastics, accounting for around 34% of global microplastic pollution. Wash your clothes from synthetic fibers less/ on gentler programs/ use washing machine filters to reduce plastic pollution. Another tip: avoid drying which uses a lot of electricity. [https://www.fashionrevolution.org/our-clothes-shed-microfibres-heres-what-we-can-do/]
- Don’t fall for greenwashing! Words like eco-friendly or sustainable mean nothing if they’re not supported by some straightforward facts. For example OEKO-TEX standard 100 is a label which only means that there are no harmful chemicals in the product when it’s sold. [https://www.oeko-tex.com/en/our-standards/oeko-tex-standard-100/] There's no assurance if workers get paid a living wage, if the company uses child labor, if the materials are good for the environment, if nothing was tested on animals etc.
- Pick up trash and recycle it. It is certainly nicer to look at woods and meadows without pieces of plastics, metals or paper lying everywhere. I believe we should normalize picking up trash – it shouldn't be something to be ashamed of. 
- Use rainwater to flower your plants. You can even collect your excess shower water with a shower bucket. 
- Keep in mind that a shower can use anywhere from 2 to 5 gallons (7 to 18 litres!) [https://www.unitconverters.net/volume/gallons-to-liters.htm] of water per minute. [https://www.roswell.enmu.edu/take-shorter-showers/] If you wash 5 times a week for 5 minutes (your shower head uses about 12 l of water/ min), you use about 300 litres of water per week. 
- Installing a low-flow showerhead or low-flush toilet is a great option to reduce your water usage as well.
- Wash only full loads in your washing machine. [https://thewaterproject.org/water_conservation_tips]

### MINDSET:
- Don’t be more sustainable for the looks/ aesthetics! That is not the right motivation. This isn’t a trend you should take on and then forget, this is an action to help the environment, animals and people. 
- Use what you have! You don’t have to buy a new pretty reusable bottle, bag etc. to be environmentally friendly – it’s better to reuse the bottle you have than buying new. The same goes with devices – if it works, use it. Research in Germany at Wuppertal Institute suggests that extending smartphone lifespans to five to seven years could halve the greenhouse gas emissions associated with these devices. [https://www.cleanenergywire.org/news/increasing-smartphone-lifespans-least-five-years-could-halve-emissions-devices-report]
- Don’t overconsume, use only what you really need. You can overconsume even the “best environmental” materials such as bamboo – the fact that it’s called “eco-friendly” material isn’t your excuse to overconsume it. 



# 🛒 Overconsumption
- Check second-hand stores, Vinted, Fb marketplace, Reknihy or borrow from friends before buying new things – this works with clothes, books (even textbooks), electronics (search for “refurbished” phones, tablets, computers,...), everyday items, gardening tools
- You can also try “no-spend month”. This challenge is about not spending money on a certain category of things (clothes, beauty products, etc. or everything) which aren’t necessary for survival. This makes you aware of your spending habits and often, you will realise that at the end of the month, you don’t want the thing you wanted at the start. 
- Try extending the lifespan of your devices. The website iFixit [https://www.ifixit.com/] focuses on repair guides for electronics, appliances, and gadgets. If you can’t fix it yourself, you can visit a Repair Café.
- Another great app is called Good On You – it makes impact analysis on fashion brands. It also writes helpful articles to learn about this topic.  [https://goodonyou.eco/]
- Support circular businesses which design products for durability, repairability, and eventual recycling or repurposing. [https://pacecircular.org/action-agenda/electronics]

### MINDSET: 
- Value experiences over items. When giving gifts, experience gifts are a great option. After all, memories are forever! Unlike most items…  
- Ignore trends. It doesn’t suit you in the long run – it depletes your money, time and the environment as well. Trends come and go and your true authenticity shouldn't be that way. Find your personal style with clothing. Buy something you will like for longer – make clothes an investment for the future. Delete apps for fast fashion clothes and unfollow fast fashion creators that tempt you to buy things. 
- Find hobbies outside online spaces. Be more outside or at least not on social media. This will lower the amount of ads you see – as in nature, there aren’t many. 
- For me, sustainability goes in hand with creating as well. You can learn so many things – sewing/ mending your clothes, upcycling, repairing broken items, cooking foods “from scratch”, growing vegetables, making homemade cleaning products,...
- Think clearly before you buy – don’t buy things in a rush. Choose quality over quantity and find your personal style with clothes. 
- Documentary: The True Cost



# 🍎 Agriculture 
- Eat less meat (start small, try for example Meatless Mondays). A report published in Science in 2018 revealed that: “A vegan diet is probably the single biggest way to reduce your impact on planet Earth, not just greenhouse gases, but global acidification, eutrophication, land use and water use. It is far bigger than cutting down on your flights or buying an electric car.” [https://unfccc.int/news/we-need-to-talk-about-meat] 
- Try vegetarian/ vegan dishes at restaurants, experiment with vegetarian/ vegan alternatives – you don’t have to be vegan to eat vegan products.  
- Buy eggs from free range chickens and meat from the Czech Republic. 
- In cosmetic products, search for cruelty free labels (= no testing on animals) such as Leaping Bunny.
- Bonus points for starting a compost (can be done even in a flat) and further reducing your food waste! 
- Buy local foods. You can even lean into seasonal eating – eat what our country produces during different seasons. This can connect you more to nature. Or start your own small garden! You can do it even on your balcony or terrace. 
- Use apps like Too Good To Go to rescue surplus food from local stores. 

### MINDSET: 
- Appreciate your meals more, realize where it came from, what it was before it ended on your plate. Eat consciously. 



# 🌳 Deforestation
- Avoid products with palm oil, otherwise look for labels such as Rainforest Alliance or  RSPO (Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil) [https://www.esmmagazine.com/supply-chain/ecolabel-use-for-certified-sustainable-palm-oil-on-the-rise-223192]. 
- Buy furniture secondhand (flea market, secondhands, FB Marketplace, Vinted, your neighbour etc.). Or at least look for some labels of sustainable forestry (but even those aren’t perfect and doesn’t mean that the wood doesn’t come from illegal loggers from protected areas), for example FSC (Forest Stewardship Council).  [https://fsc.org/en]
- Ditch paper towels in the kitchen, use old fabrics or t-shirts. 
- Wrap gifts in newspaper or fabrics.
- Use Ecosia (yes, another reason). It plants the right species of trees in the right places and takes care of them for a longer time (I know lots of companies promise to plant trees when you e.g. make a purchase, but sometimes, it’s not in natural environment for the certain tree, or the tree is taken down after few months). Ecosia also puts out an annual report at the end of the year commenting on the use of their finances. [https://blog.ecosia.org/ecosia-financial-reports-tree-planting-receipts/]
- For Christmas, buy a tree from the local forest (did you know most trees you buy for Christmas are from Denmark? [https://www.csr.dk/danish-christmas-trees-germany-hong-kong]). Some foresters sell cheap trees which they cut down to make space for the other trees – last year (2025), this worked in hájovna Vrbětín (around 15km from Benešov).There will certainly be other foresters which do this, you just have to search for a while. 
- You can also donate to some organizations that fight against deforestation, but be careful – check if they do what they promise. 

### MINDSET: 
- This solution is the easiest, cheapest and most convenient: if you don’t need it, don’t buy it! Please don’t overconsume just because something is trendy  - trends come and go. A lot of furniture can be found secondhand or on flea markets. Choose the product with history.  
- Buy a plant and take care of it (the best is to buy seeds and watch how it grows). You can find plant seeds even on swaps or get some from your friends or family. Or start your small garden with microgreens, strawberries, herbs or small vegetables. You can do that even on your balcony. 
- Be in nature to remind yourself what you’re fighting for. 



# 🦋 Biodiversity Loss 
- Choose carefully your wildlife tourism options – captive wild animals are often caged, drugged or tied down for our viewing and petting pleasure. Living with humans for sure isn’t their natural habitat – so don’t take selfies with tigers, dolphins, etc. don’t pay to pet them and don’t support their shows.  It serves no conservation purpose, it’s usually only for profit. [https://www.smithsonianmag.com/science-nature/the-big-unsexy-problem-with-tiger-selfies-180964489/]
- Don’t buy exotic animal skin or fur.
- Don’t purchase wildlife products, souvenirs or bushmeat – just because people sell it, doesn’t mean it’s legal, safe or ethical.
- Don’t eat whales, tigers, pangolins and other endangered species.
- Be responsible on social media – don’t support content with wild animals in unnatural surroundings, social media activity is noticed by traders. 

### MINDSET: 
- Think more about animals and their life conditions.
- Sign petitions.
